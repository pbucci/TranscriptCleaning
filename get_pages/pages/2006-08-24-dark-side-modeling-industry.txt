			<h2><a href="http://www.foxnews.com/on-air/oreilly/index.html">Interviews</a></h2>
			<h1 itemprop="headline">The Dark Side of the Modeling Industry</h1>
			<div class="article-info">
			<div> 		
			 		<time itemprop="datePublished" pubdate datetime="2006-08-24T11:11-04:00">Published August 24, 2006</time><div itemprop="sourceOrganization" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Organization">FOX News</div></div>
			</div>		
			
			<div class="social-count">
					<div>
		                <a class="fbk" href="#"><span>Facebook</span></a><span class="fbk">0</span>
		                <a class="twt" href="#"><span>Twitter</span></a><span class="twt">0</span>
		                <a class="email" href="#"><span>Email</span></a>
			            <a class="print" href="#"><span>Print</span></a>

	            </div>           
	            
            <div itemprop="articleBody">			
			 <p>This is a <em>partial</em> transcript from <em>&quot;<strong>The O'Reilly Factor</strong>,&quot;</em> August 23, 2006, that has been edited for clarity.</p>
<p><em>Watch</em> <strong>&quot;The O'Reilly Factor&quot;</strong> <em>weeknights at 8 p.m. and 11 p.m. ET and listen to the</em> <strong>&quot;Radio Factor!&quot;</strong></p>
<p><strong>BILL O'REILLY, HOST:</strong> In the &quot;Back of the Book&quot; segment tonight, on the surface modeling looks like a glamorous industry, but over the years, there have been a number of sordid scandals involving super models. For example, <b>Naomi Campbell</b> has been arrested a number of times. She's currently facing charges of assault. <b>Kate Moss</b> got in trouble with cocaine during a turbulent relationship with a British punk rocker.</p>
<p><b>Christie Brinkley</b> has had in trouble in her personal life finding men she can trust. And Gia, a supermodel in the late '70s, became a drug addict and died from AIDS.</p>
<p>With us now is model Robin Hazelwood, a graduate of <b>Yale University</b> and author of the book &quot;Model Student: A Tale of Co-eds and Cover Girls&quot;.</p>
<p>Reading your bio, you got into the modeling industry at age 14, which is not unusual, right?</p>
<p><strong>ROBIN HAZELWOOD, AUTHOR, &quot;MODEL STUDENT&quot;:</strong> No. I would say that's the most — 14, 15 is the most common time.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> Is that the reason that a lot of these girls go wild, because they're immature and they're unsupervised over in <b>Milan</b> and <b>Paris</b> and New York City here, and they just succumb to temptation?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> Yes. I think that does have a lot to do with it. What happens is the agencies find these girls quite young. The story that that they tell the parents is, &quot;Have your daughter come, you know, a nice summer abroad. We are bookers, you know. The agents will stay with them, will look after them. They will be chaperoned. And then often they go to these cities — and it's not exactly like that.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> Look, you've got photographers. You've got young people in proximity to each other. You know what's going to happen. But didn't your parents — I mean, you're a Midwest girl. You're from Milwaukee, right?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> Yes.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> OK. Didn't they know that having you at age 14 in this business was kind of risky?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> Well, I didn't come to New York at 14. To be fair, what happens typically is for the first year or two, I would say, a girl is sort of cutting her teeth in a smaller local market.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> You were in Chicago.</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> I was in Chicago. She doesn't usually come to a big city like New York, Paris or Milan until the ripe old age of 16.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> Sixteen! </p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> But, keep in mind, what happens, there's a little bit of collusion. The agents are telling the parents, you know, the whole thing I just said about the chaperones. And the child, who wants to model, because she thinks it's quite a glamorous thing, is typically...</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> Was it fun for you?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> At first I thought it was fun. And it was glamorous. But of course, I didn't come home to my parents and say, &quot;Oh, by the way, I saw a model doing some 'blow' in the bathroom today.&quot;</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> Why didn't you do cocaine? Or did you?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> I didn't do it. I didn't do it to the extent that most models do, I would say, and that my character in the book does. But I included a lot of it in my book because I feel like it's typical.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> You did the cocaine a little in this stuff?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> It's -- the industry is full of it.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> But why didn't you get addicted like Kate Moss? Was there something holding you back from going over the edge?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> I definitely, you know, had my head screwed on straight.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> You did?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> Possibly to the detriment of my modeling career, I would say.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> You think so?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> Sure! Absolutely.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> You wouldn't do certain things that people wanted you to do, sleep with them and stuff?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> Yes, definitely an agency. You know, it's an industry where, you know, for example, in Milan, a lot of the agencies are owned by these wealthy individuals — these men.</p>
<p>And you get there and the moment you get there, the bookers are saying, &quot;Come to dinner. Come to dinner.&quot; You show up at dinner and a string of 50-year-old men sitting next to 15-year-old girls. It's sort of understood that it's going to help your career if that dinner extends into the night. </p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> What percentage of the young girls go into the drugs and go with the guys, would you say?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> High. Very high.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> Fifty, 60, 70? What would you say?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> Yes. I would say 60 to 70 percent.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> Really? That many?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> You have to understand, there's a huge attrition rate. Every summer that I modeled and for the years afterwards, I can't tell you how many girls I encountered and I worked with for a summer. And you never really saw them again. They either, you know...</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> Yeah, they wash out, they don't make it.</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> They wash out. See, you know, in Milan, a lot of them, they start having these affairs with people. Or you know, the bulimia. I mean, it's — there's a lot of pressure put on people at such a young age.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> Did you have any eating disorders?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> I did not have any eating disorders but I would say at that time I was extremely preoccupied with every calorie that went into my mouth.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> Did you eat nutritionally, or did you just, like, have a salad once a day?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> I wouldn't say, you know, I ate as nutritionally as would be optimal.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> So it's an obsession to keep yourself reed thin.</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> It's very hard to be thin naturally.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> Drugs are everywhere?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> Yes.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> And guys are trying to hit on you 24/7?</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> Yes.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> All right. I think, look, people should read your book, and parents should absolutely know this is the industry, this is the modeling industry. Because I know some models and they say exactly what you're saying.</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> It's true. And yet, there's still — you know, people still seem to think it's so glamorous.</p>
<p><b>O'REILLY:</b> Right. All right.</p>
<p>Well, we appreciate you coming in, Robin. Thank you.</p>
<p><b>HAZELWOOD:</b> Thank you for having me.</p>
