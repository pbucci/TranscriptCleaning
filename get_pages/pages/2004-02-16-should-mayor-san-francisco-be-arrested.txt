			<h2><a href="http://www.foxnews.com/on-air/oreilly/index.html">Interviews</a></h2>
			<h1 itemprop="headline">Should the Mayor of San Francisco be Arrested?</h1>
			<div class="article-info">
			<div> 		
			 		<time itemprop="datePublished" pubdate datetime="2004-02-16T11:20-05:00">Published February 16, 2004</time><div itemprop="sourceOrganization" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Organization">FOX News</div></div>
			</div>		
			
			<div class="social-count">
					<div>
		                <a class="fbk" href="#"><span>Facebook</span></a><span class="fbk">0</span>
		                <a class="twt" href="#"><span>Twitter</span></a><span class="twt">0</span>

            <div itemprop="articleBody">			
			 <p>This is a <i>partial</i> transcript from &quot;The O'Reilly Factor,&quot;&nbsp;February 13, 2004 that has been edited for clarity.</p>
<p><em>Watch</em> The O'Reilly Factor <em>weeknights at 8 p.m. and 11 p.m. ET and</em> listen <em>to</em> the Radio Factor<em>!</em></p>
<p><strong>BILL O'REILLY,&nbsp;HOST</strong>:&nbsp;&nbsp;Now for the top story tonight, another view of this from the senior judicial analyst at Fox News, Judge Andrew Napolitano.</p>
<p>Am I going terribly wrong here?</p>
<p><strong>ANDREW NAPOLITANO, FOX NEWS SR. JUDICIAL ANALYST</strong>:&nbsp; No, I think you're about 95 percent right.&nbsp; I agree with everything you said, except the issue of arresting the mayor.</p>
<p>Now the mayor of San Francisco can no more issue marriage licenses than he can medical licenses.&nbsp; It's just not a function of him or a function of the city.&nbsp; In that particular state, marriage has been already defined by a power greater than the legislature, the people at a referendum.&nbsp; So...&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Yes, but he's disturbing the peace here.&nbsp; Why not arrest him?&nbsp;</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; Well, he's disturbing the peace...</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; So why not arrest him -- I don't think he'd get convicted.</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; No, no.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; With a symbolic gesture, go on in and say look, you're abusing the privilege of your office, you're wasting the taxpayers' money on this, you're disturbing the peace, because this is huge.</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; Yes.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; And I'm going to have you arrested.&nbsp;</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; You know what?&nbsp; They probably want their money wasted in that town.&nbsp; The laws are so crazy.&nbsp; The government pays $50,000 for a government employee to have a same-sex operation change.&nbsp; They probably think he's doing the right thing.</p>
<p>He's mis-serving his own people, because the people that think they're married, the people that he thinks he has married, will have those marriages undone.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; (UNINTELLIGIBLE).</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; It's a meaningless document...</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Right.</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; ...because the city doesn't have the power to grant it.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; It's a symbolic gesture -- it's a symbolic middle finger to the country, all right?&nbsp; But I want to send another symbolic middle finger right back to Newsom...</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; In the form of handcuffs.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; ...and put him in cuffs, and take him away, and say look, we have laws.&nbsp; We're a nation of laws.&nbsp; You have disturbed the peace by violating one of our laws.&nbsp; And we are going to hold you accountable.&nbsp;</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; The traditional interpretations of disturbing the peace do not justify arresting him here.&nbsp; And we don't have common law arrests.&nbsp; You can't just arrest somebody because they have done a bizarre or weird thing.&nbsp; You have to violate a specific statute.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Couldn't you find a statute here to arrest that this guy?</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; You know I tried this afternoon, Bill, because your team, that asked me to be here with you, asked me to try.&nbsp; And I couldn't find one.&nbsp; But he can be sued for malfeasance.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; He's already being sued.&nbsp;</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; He can be sued.&nbsp; And a judge can call him, activist or not, should enjoin what he's doing.&nbsp; It is clear that he doesn't have the power to do it, that he's making a mockery of the government, and that he's mis-serving the people he claims he's helping.&nbsp; Politically, he's very popular.&nbsp; He was just elected.&nbsp; He's got four more years before he has to confront the electorate.&nbsp; And the electorate in San Francisco probably likes what he's trying to do.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Well, of course they do.&nbsp; He's playing to his crowd.&nbsp; But what he's doing is he's undermining the rule of law in this country.&nbsp; And that's&nbsp; happening all over the place.&nbsp;</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; He is.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Basically, undermining the rule of law.</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; Absolutely.&nbsp; You gave a long and poignant litany in your opening statement about undermining the rule of law.&nbsp; This is just another example.&nbsp; And it's curious.&nbsp; The left, the progressive groups, the ones that wanted Roy Moore's head on a dish...</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; You bet.</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; ...are absolutely silent.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Yes, you can undermine the law if it's a law they don't like.&nbsp; All right...</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; You can be in favor of gay marriage and still say the&nbsp; mayor's doing the wrong thing, because he doesn't have the power to do it.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Now one of the things -- sure.&nbsp; But that's not usually the way&nbsp; those people operate.</p>
<p>Now one of the things I cited was the judge is attempting -- Virginia -- to undermine the partial birth abortion ban.&nbsp; All right?&nbsp; Now the federal government understands that this is going to happen now all over the place.</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; Right.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; So [Attorney General John] Ashcroft wants to subpoena medical records to show that&nbsp;<b>partial-birth abortions</b> (<a target="_blank" href="http://clickit.go2net.com/search?cid=307797&amp;site=srch&amp;area=is.clicktracking&amp;shape=link&amp;cp=info.foxnws&amp;clickurl=http%3A%2F%2Fsearch.foxnews.com%2Finfo.foxnws%2Fredirs_all.htm%3Fpgtarg%3Dwbsdogpile&amp;ext_qcat=web&amp;ext_qkw=partial-birth%20abortions">search</a>)&nbsp;in many cases have been done on healthy babies by the&nbsp; request of healthy women.&nbsp; Correct?</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; Yes.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; All right.&nbsp; That's -- he held a press conference.&nbsp; And look, we want to prove that this partial birth abortion ruse, where they say it's the health of the woman, that you can find a doctor who will kill a baby for absolutely no reason because the mother just doesn't want it.</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; Right.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Now the judges say no, you can't have those medical records proving that because it's a violation of privacy.&nbsp; What say you?</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; OK, I say the&nbsp;<b>Justice Department</b> (<a target="_blank" href="http://clickit.go2net.com/search?cid=307797&amp;site=srch&amp;area=is.clicktracking&amp;shape=link&amp;cp=info.foxnws&amp;clickurl=http%3A%2F%2Fsearch.foxnews.com%2Finfo.foxnws%2Fredirs_all.htm%3Fpgtarg%3Dwbsdogpile&amp;ext_qcat=web&amp;ext_qkw=Justice%20Department">search</a>)&nbsp;is doing exactly the right thing.&nbsp; The doctors who have challenged the partial birth abortion ban have said it's for medically necessary reasons.&nbsp; So the Justice Department has said writing out the names and other identifying features of your patients, let's see your records so that we can evaluate in the presence of a judge whether those partial birth abortions or whatever you want to call them, doctor, were medically necessary.&nbsp; Even though the statues enforcement has been stayed by&nbsp; two federal judges, the issue of the subpoena has been ruled on differently.</p>
<p>A judge in Chicago said the statute stayed.&nbsp; No subpoena.&nbsp; A judge here in Manhattan said to the doctors, wait a minute, you guys challenged the statute.&nbsp; You claim this is interfering with your professional judgment.&nbsp; Prove it.&nbsp; Let the Justice Department see the records.&nbsp; The Justice Department doesn't care about the...&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; All right, so you have dueling -- for judges now, you have...</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; That is correct.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; ...you know, the usual activist judges, those who don't like the procedure probably rule in favor of the Justice Department.&nbsp; Those who want&nbsp; partial birth abortion rule against it.&nbsp;</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; Unfortunately, that's right.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Yes.&nbsp; What we are degenerating into here, judge, is anarchy.&nbsp;</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; We are, to an extent we're getting to an point in our society where justice is determined by the personality and the attitude of the judges.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Not the law.</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; Not the law.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Not the will of the people.&nbsp;</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; Not the will of the people.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Right.</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; Not basic human liberty guaranteed by the Constitution.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Right.</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; And that's wrong.&nbsp; The most poignant example is what you pointed out in San Francisco.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; All right.</p>
<p>NAPOLITANO:&nbsp; It's got to be stopped.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; OK, Judge Andrew Napolitano, thank you very much.</p>
