			<h2><a href="http://www.foxnews.com/on-air/oreilly/index.html">Interviews</a></h2>
			<h1 itemprop="headline">The 'Social Promotion' Debate: Who's Looking Out for the Kids?</h1>
			<div class="article-info">
			<div> 		
			 		<time itemprop="datePublished" pubdate datetime="2004-03-18T12:51-05:00">Published March 18, 2004</time><div itemprop="sourceOrganization" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Organization">FOX News</div></div>

		                <a class="fbk" href="#"><span>Facebook</span></a><span class="fbk">0</span>

			 <p>This is a <i>partial</i> transcript from &quot;The O'Reilly Factor,&quot;&nbsp;March 17, 2004 that has been edited for clarity.</p>
<p><em>Watch</em> The O'Reilly Factor <em>weeknights at 8 p.m. and 11 p.m. ET and</em> listen <em>to</em> the Radio Factor<em>!</em></p>
<p><strong>BILL O'REILLY,&nbsp;HOST</strong>:&nbsp; In the &quot;Impact&quot; segment tonight,&nbsp;<b>New York City Mayor Michael Bloomberg</b> (<a target="_blank" href="http://clickit.go2net.com/search?cid=307797&amp;site=srch&amp;area=is.clicktracking&amp;shape=link&amp;cp=info.foxnws&amp;clickurl=http%3A%2F%2Fsearch.foxnews.com%2Finfo.foxnws%2Fredirs_all.htm%3Fpgtarg%3Dwbsdogpile&amp;ext_qcat=web&amp;ext_qkw=New%20York%20City%20Mayor%20Michael%20Bloomberg">search</a>)&nbsp;has fired three members of the public school board who would not vote to stop social promotion.&nbsp;The mayor wants third graders who cannot read and do basic math to be left back, but &quot;The New York Times&quot; and others say that's a bad policy because it&nbsp; hurts a child's self-esteem and may lead to even more failure.</p>
<p>With us now is&nbsp;<b>Dr. Herman Sirois</b> (<a target="_blank" href="http://clickit.go2net.com/search?cid=307797&amp;site=srch&amp;area=is.clicktracking&amp;shape=link&amp;cp=info.foxnws&amp;clickurl=http%3A%2F%2Fsearch.foxnews.com%2Finfo.foxnws%2Fredirs_all.htm%3Fpgtarg%3Dwbsdogpile&amp;ext_qcat=web&amp;ext_qkw=Dr.%20Herman%20Sirois">search</a>), the superintendent of the Levittown public school system.&nbsp; Levittown on Long Island, as you may, is where I grew up.</p>
<p>All right, now you are for social promotion, is that right?</p>
<p><strong>HERMAN SIROIS, Ph.D., LEVITTOWN NY SUPERINTENDENT</strong>:&nbsp; No, we're not for social promotion at all.&nbsp; We don't necessarily believe social promotion exists.&nbsp; That's not the issue at all.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; All right, but if they can't pass the basic test, and it's a really easy test, and 29,000 of them out of 72,000 couldn't...</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Right.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; ...what do you do with these kids, put them in the fourth grade when they can't read?</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Well, if you look at any grade in any school in&nbsp;&nbsp; America, it's not a grade level.&nbsp; It's an age level.&nbsp; And the groups of students from second grade to sixth, even seventh grade abilities in&nbsp; fourth grade, every teacher is trained to teach all the children that are there at that grade level.&nbsp; This social promotion is a...</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; But - see, I was a former teacher.&nbsp; And I'm putting myself now in the fourth grade.&nbsp; In the fourth grade, you're supposed to be able to read.&nbsp; All right, you have to read.&nbsp; You have to read the textbooks.&nbsp; You have to read.&nbsp; If you can't read, the teacher can't possibly teach you.&nbsp;</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Oh, no, that's not true.&nbsp; It's a teacher's job to teach you.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; How can a teacher with 25 kids in a class, teach three kids who can't read the geography book?</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; If you're going to retain students in any grade, you have to make sure that those students are going to get a better education in the grade you're retaining them than they would in the next grade.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; All right.</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; If not, you're only telling them they're going to fail.&nbsp; And you're promoting failure.&nbsp; There's nothing in the research in our&nbsp; business that ever indicated that retention is a benefit to the children.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; All right, so you -- if the kid can't read --&nbsp; I mean, this is how I want to boil it down to you.</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Right.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; It's almost like a see Dick run here, doctor, OK?&nbsp; If you're telling me, if a kid in the third grade can't read, can't pass this test, you're going to put him in the fourth grade, doctor?</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Absolutely.&nbsp; If the child is doing the best that child can...</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; If he's trying the hardest he can...</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Trying the hardest he can...</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; ...but he can't read, you're putting him up there?</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Then it's the fourth grade teacher's responsibility.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; And what if the fourth grade teacher doesn't teach him how to read?</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Then it'll be the fifth grade teacher's responsibility.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; So you're going to just move them along?</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; You can't have 16-year-olds in third grade.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; No, but you're just moving - you're warehousing these kids.&nbsp; You're putting them on a little assembly line.&nbsp; They're going woop...</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; No.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; And then what is the incentive?</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Let's take warehousing.&nbsp; You're saying we're warehousing because you're warehousing the kids if you leave them in third grade.&nbsp; Because eventually they're just going to quit.&nbsp; I don't think he's going to have...</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; No, that's not true.&nbsp; I don't believe eventually they're just going to quit.&nbsp;</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Of course they will.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; No, they're not.</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Why would you stay in third grade for four years?</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Because if you leaven them back -- and nobody likes to be left back.&nbsp; That's a stigma and it's a tough thing.&nbsp;</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; And how many times would you leave them back?</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; All right, I would leave them back and then put them into a separate category of we've got to teach this kid to read and -- or do the basic math.&nbsp;</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Why don't you do that now?</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; These children - look...</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Why hold them back?&nbsp; Why ruin their self-image?</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; ...this - I've seen this New York City test.&nbsp; If these kids can't do it in the third grade, they're never going to succeed.&nbsp; What do you think, some miracle's going to happen, that they're going to learn how to read in the fourth grade and learn how to add&nbsp;&nbsp; four and four?</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Do you think a miracle's going to happen in third grade?</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; If you isolate the children, segregate them out, and give them the phonics game or whatever they need, that's...</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; You've already given them that.&nbsp; Here's the reason it's being done, let's face the facts.&nbsp; If you keep these kids in third grade, they won't take the fourth grade test.&nbsp; If they don't take the fourth grade test, the fourth graders will do better on the test.&nbsp; If they do better on the test, the system will look like it will have improved.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; I understand there's politics involved.&nbsp;</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; It's a sham from the beginning.&nbsp; It is...</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; But social promotion has been a disaster in Los&nbsp; Angeles.&nbsp; It's been a disaster in New York City.&nbsp;</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; That's not true.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Yes, it is.</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; It has not been a social&nbsp; disaster.&nbsp; As a matter of fact, you have children who attended the...</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Are you going to tell me, doctor, that kids who can get out of high school and can't read...</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; You had kids in New York City...</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; ...effectively and can't write a paragraph, that this is effective schooling?</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; It's effective management of children if they can't do anything else.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; (UNINTELLIGIBLE).&nbsp; Who's looking out for those kids?&nbsp; If you're looking out for them if you impose standards and discipline on them?</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; And send them out of school at early ages, make them quit school as soon as...</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; You don't make anybody do anything.&nbsp; You have to get parental permission to quit school.&nbsp; You know that.</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; At 16, at 18?&nbsp; No.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Yes.&nbsp; At 16 is the lowest you can quit school.&nbsp;</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Oh, you know this, and I'm sure you're up on a lot of the research, if you retain children, it will lead to dropping out.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; There's a higher rate, but it's only about 12 percent&nbsp; higher.&nbsp; Look, I'm telling you, you're not looking out for the kids if you don't be demand performance.&nbsp; Look, the reason kids can't...</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; I'm telling -- you're not looking out for the kids unless you do something to guarantee their&nbsp; success.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Guarantee?&nbsp; You can't guarantee.</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Then how are you going to guarantee it in third grade, if you don't guarantee it in fourth grade?</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Do you know why they can't read?&nbsp; They can't read because of their parents, that's why.&nbsp;</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; And are we doing anything about that?</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; What, are you going to break into their house?</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; How about the crime in the street?&nbsp; Are we doing anything about the poverty?&nbsp; 45 percent of these kids are living their lives in poverty.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; So what do you want to do?</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; What do we do?&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Give them more money?&nbsp; Do you want to go to&nbsp; their house and give them money?</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; You end up punishing the victim.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; I'm not punishing anybody.&nbsp; I'm holding them accountable.&nbsp; The public school system has to have standards and accountability.&nbsp;</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; In fourth grade?</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Yes.</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Third grade?</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; In first grade.</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Why only third grade?</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Look, look at the parochial schools.</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Whoa, why only third grade?</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; No, the third grade is the litmus test because at that point a kid can understand the test.&nbsp;</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; The third grade is being implemented in third grade so these kids don't have to take the fourth grade test.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; No.</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; It's a pure political...</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Doctor, we have a gentleman's disagreement here.&nbsp; But I'm telling you, if your kid can't read in the third grade, it's his parents' fault.&nbsp; And the school has to take emergency measures by giving that kid attention.</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Hold it.&nbsp; Now first, we blame the schools.&nbsp; Now we're going to blame just the parents.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; I'm not blaming the schools.&nbsp; I'm blaming the parents, flat out blaming them.&nbsp; If your kid can't read in the third grade, it's your fault.</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; No.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Social policies, bologna.&nbsp; This isn't Cuba.&nbsp; We can't force people to be...</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Forty-five percent of our children living in poverty, and you got a tax break and I got a tax break...</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; Forty five percent of our children don't live in poverty.</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Forty five percent.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; OK?&nbsp; Poor kids are up against it, but this is a capitalistic system.&nbsp; And that's the way it always will be.&nbsp; You work your way out of poverty.&nbsp; You educate yourself.&nbsp;</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; No, this is not a capitalist society.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; No, what is it?</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; Our society is unbridled capitalism.&nbsp; That's the source of most of these kids' problems.&nbsp;</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; All right, you're right on capitalism.&nbsp; But you sound like a socialist to me, doctor.&nbsp; And I'm -- from coming from Levittown, that's really a shock.&nbsp; But I appreciate your point of view.&nbsp;</p>
<p>SIROIS:&nbsp; (UNINTELLIGIBLE) call me names.&nbsp; You know that.</p>
<p>O'REILLY:&nbsp; I'm not.&nbsp; I just said you sound like a socialist, that's all I said.&nbsp; We appreciate your point of view.</p>
