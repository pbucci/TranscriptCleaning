			<h2><a href="http://www.foxnews.com/on-air/oreilly/index.html">Interviews</a></h2>
			<h1 itemprop="headline">New Information About Nick Berg</h1>
			<div class="article-info">
			<div> 		
			 		<time itemprop="datePublished" pubdate datetime="2004-05-17T12:12-04:00">Published May 17, 2004</time><div itemprop="sourceOrganization" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Organization">FOX News</div></div>
			</div>		
			
			<div class="social-count">
					<div>
		                <a class="fbk" href="#"><span>Facebook</span></a><span class="fbk">0</span>
		                <a class="twt" href="#"><span>Twitter</span></a><span class="twt">0</span>

            <div itemprop="articleBody">			
			 <p>This is a <i>partial</i> transcript from &quot;The O'Reilly Factor,&quot;&nbsp;May 14, 2004 that has been edited for clarity.</p>
<p><em>Watch</em> The O'Reilly Factor <em>weeknights at 8 p.m. and 11 p.m. ET and</em> listen <em>to</em> the Radio Factor<em>!</em></p>
<p><strong>TONY SNOW, GUEST&nbsp;HOST:</strong>&nbsp; But first, our top story tonight.&nbsp; American businessman Nick Berg, whose brutal beheading in Iraq shocked the nation this week, was laid to rest today in Westchester, Pennsylvania.&nbsp; But there's still a host of unanswered questions about his detention in Iraq and the bizarre link between Berg and Zacarias Moussaoui, the man known as the 20th 9/11 hijacker.</p>
<p>Joining us now from Washington, Elaine Shannon, an investigative reporter from &quot;Time&quot; magazine looking into the story.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Elaine, you look at this story and there are a lot of pieces that really don't seem to fit.&nbsp;Let's start first with the link to Zacarias Moussaoui.&nbsp; Most people are now saying just an innocent theft of an e-mail address.</p>
<p><strong>ELAINE SHANNON, TIME MAGAZINE</strong>:&nbsp; That was the FBI's&nbsp; conclusion. Zacarias Moussaoui lived in Norman, Oklahoma from February 2001 till late August 2001, when he left to go to Minnesota where he was arrested.&nbsp; In tracking back Moussaoui's actions in the United States to see if he had associates, the FBI spent a lot of time&nbsp; looking at people he'd met at the mosque in Norman, people he had encountered in other places, and who they paled around with, to see if there was a sleeper cell that he was associated with.&nbsp; And in the course of this, they found somebody that had used Berg's e-mail.&nbsp;</p>
<p><b>SNOW</b>:&nbsp; OK, they -- but at this point, there's no link between the two.&nbsp; In fact, the FBI at least at this point has disavowed it.&nbsp; What do you...</p>
<p><b>SHANNON</b>:&nbsp; They interviewed him.&nbsp; And he explained he'd met a guy on the bus going between classes, and that guy wanted access to - e-mail access to the Internet.&nbsp; So he let him use his e-mail address.&nbsp; This guy took the e-mail and gave it to a lot of other people.&nbsp; And so, it got kind of spread around.&nbsp; The guy, I'm told, had a&nbsp; connection to the flight school where Moussaoui ended up.</p>
<p><b>SNOW</b>:&nbsp; OK.</p>
<p><b>SHANNON</b>:&nbsp; So there were some students there that probably had this e-mail.&nbsp; And that's how it came up.&nbsp;</p>
<p><b>SNOW</b>:&nbsp; Help us out a little bit also with Nick Berg's detention in Iraq.&nbsp; Apparently he was detained by Iraqi officials riding around in a cab.&nbsp; But apparently he had on his person, I want you to confirm or deny, a copy of the Koran and an anti-Semitic tract?&nbsp; I mean, here you've got a Jewish-American in Iraq.&nbsp; What's the deal?</p>
<p><b>SHANNON</b>:&nbsp; I read that.&nbsp; I don't know -- I can't confirm that on my own and know that he had a passport with an Israeli stamp in it.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; He told associates about that.&nbsp; And he also was carrying around a lot of electronics equipment because he liked to repair communications towers.&nbsp; And that may have been enough for the Iraqi police to take him in.</p>
<p>Of course, you know there's -- the family thinks that the U.S.&nbsp;&nbsp; military was holding him, not the Iraqi police.&nbsp;</p>
<p><b>SNOW</b>:&nbsp; Right.&nbsp; And at this point, nobody could get to the bottom of that.&nbsp; Or can they?</p>
<p><b>SHANNON</b>:&nbsp; Well, there's two sides to this.</p>
<p><b>SNOW</b>:&nbsp; OK.</p>
<p><b>SHANNON</b>:&nbsp; The family believes that the military is telling police&nbsp; what to do.&nbsp; The police are saying, well, we didn't have him.&nbsp; The military had him.&nbsp; There's an e-mail that said the military had him.&nbsp; The FBI says, well, we talked to him, but we didn't tell them to keep him there.</p>
<p><b>SNOW</b>:&nbsp; Right.</p>
<p><b>SHANNON</b>:&nbsp; And in fact, we decided he was OK and they should let him out, and let him out of the country.&nbsp;</p>
<p><b>SNOW</b>:&nbsp; But there are a lot of pieces here, as I said before, that don't fit or at least seem a little bit odd.&nbsp; Does anybody have any clue&nbsp; exactly what he was doing in Iraq?&nbsp; I know we've been told he wanted to build radio towers.&nbsp; But the company he owns isn't incorporated.&nbsp; There are no incorporation papers.&nbsp; He makes his way over there.&nbsp; What was he doing?</p>
<p><b>SHANNON</b>:&nbsp; Well, all the stories I've read suggest that he was having a great adventure, that he was a very bold guy.&nbsp; He was fearless.&nbsp; He enjoyed hanging out there.&nbsp; And he was hanging out.&nbsp;</p>
<p>And Iraqis, other Americans, other&nbsp; business people who were over there thought that he was very foolhardy at times to be going where he was going.&nbsp; He went to Mosul by himself without an interpreter and a bodyguard.&nbsp; And he was taking public transportation sometimes.&nbsp; And he was pretty visible, because he's a large guy.&nbsp; He had a real short haircut.&nbsp; I think people probably thought he was military.&nbsp; And it would have been easy for somebody who wanted to kidnap him.&nbsp; And in the current climate, kidnapping's pretty easy to mark him as a target.&nbsp;</p>
<p><b>SNOW</b>:&nbsp; The other thing is the military actually offered to take him home free of charge.&nbsp; He said no.&nbsp; And one of the things he&nbsp; apparently told U.S. officials&nbsp; is you don't understand these people like I do.&nbsp;</p>
<p><b>SHANNON</b>:&nbsp; Well, that's possible.&nbsp; And his father has said, well,&nbsp; he was just sick of the U.S. military and the FBI.&nbsp; He wanted nothing to do with them out of the -- after the detention.&nbsp; And he was going to make his own way.&nbsp; He was obviously a free spirit, a very independent-minded person.&nbsp;</p>
<p><b>SNOW</b>:&nbsp; Is there any -- do you have any theories at this point, other than being a free spirit over there, and taking foolish risks, any other theories about what he was&nbsp; doing?</p>
<p><b>SHANNON</b>:&nbsp; Well, you can theorize eyes all you want.&nbsp; I know that the FBI concluded he was not a spy, that he was not a new John&nbsp; Walker Linde.&nbsp; That must've crossed their mind when they had him in their files from 2002, and they'd cleared him then, but then he shows up again in a rather strange position.&nbsp; And they probably spent a lot of&nbsp; extra time checking out does he have some associates that we&nbsp; missed the first time.&nbsp;</p>
<p><b>SNOW</b>:&nbsp; All right, Elaine Shannon, thank you very much.</p>
<p><b>SHANNON</b>:&nbsp; Thank you.</p>
